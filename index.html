Climate Variability: The Impact on Ethiopia’s Economy & Persistent Poverty

Overview

The Federal Democratic Republic of Ethiopia is a landlocked country located in East Africa. As of 2025, it has a population of around 135 million. Ethiopia is not only the 10th-most populous country in the world but is also known for having one of the lowest GDPs per capita globally. Contributing to this are numerous contagious diseases that are worsened by poor sanitation and malnutrition. Over 58 million people, nearly half the population, do not have access to clean water as of 2023.

This development status makes the country more vulnerable to climate variability and change. In recent years, the environment has become a key issue in Ethiopia because it affects the country as a whole. Ethiopia has multiple climate zones that vary from hot and arid to cold and humid. However, the main environmental problems include droughts, floods, and water and air pollution, which lead to poor crop yields, land degradation, soil erosion, loss of biodiversity, and livestock depletion.

"What is the impact of climate variability on economic growth in Ethiopia, and how does it contribute to persistent poverty in an agriculture-dependent economy?"

Climate & Agriculture

Crops grown during the summer season are harvested between September and December and account for 90–95% of total annual production. The timing and amount of rainfall during the growing season are therefore critical for crop production, as 96% of cropland is rain-fed. This means that farmers rely heavily on the weather; with the climate becoming increasingly fluctuating and extreme, a bad crop equals an almost entire loss of production.

Agriculture is an essential driver of economic growth in the Ethiopian economy. Crop and livestock production account for roughly 65% and 25% of agricultural GDP, respectively. In 2020, agriculture generated 34.5% of the total GDP. Beyond products, agricultural labor makes up around 85% of the total labor force.

Experts predict warming temperatures in all four seasons, with an annual increase of 2.2°C by the 2050s, which will lead to significant reductions in agricultural production.

Education

Work and school often compete for children’s time. In a recent survey, experts found that higher rainfall during the year was negatively correlated with enrollment and grade attainment in school. This especially affected males, as boys were often pulled out of school to assist with farm labor during productive periods or to seek work after crop failures.

Findings by the Ethiopia Rural Socioeconomic Survey:

  • Among rural children, primary school enrollment was 57% for boys and 59% for girls.
  • Secondary school enrollment (ages 15-18) was approximately 2.5% for both boys and girls.
  • 71% of rural children aged 10–14 are employed, and the mean number of hours worked per week is 28.

Economy

Agriculture is an essential driver of economic growth in the Ethiopian economy. High sensitivity to rainfall fluctuations directly correlates with GDP volatility. Agriculture contributes significantly to the overall monetary value of the economy by creating employment opportunities, generating exports, and feeding the nation.

Effect & NARDL

The effects of climate change, such as major national droughts, create "poverty traps" for rural households, making it nearly impossible to build up assets or escape poverty once a shock occurs. An experiment showed that a 1% increase in annual temperature variability is found to decrease the Ethiopian annual gross domestic product (GDP) by up to 4.5%.

The Nonlinear Autoregressive Distributed Lag (NARDL) model recognizes that economic systems often respond differently to positive and negative shocks. Ethiopia's economy does not respond to "good" weather and "bad" weather in equal measure; the negative shocks from climate variability (like droughts) have a more lasting and damaging effect than the positive shocks of "good" seasons.

Summary

In Ethiopia, climate-induced reductions in crop output have led to an estimated 5%-10% decline in annual agricultural GDP. Over a 50-year period, the projected reduction in agricultural productivity due to climate change may lead to 20% to 30% less average income compared to a scenario without climate change.

  • Macro Strategy: Climate adaptation should be a core part of macroeconomic planning.
  • Poverty Cycle: Climate shocks create "poverty traps," preventing asset accumulation.

Initiative

Ethiopian agricultural businesses are set to get €40 million in financing under EIB Global accord with Zemen Bank. This focuses on climate action and environmental sustainability.
