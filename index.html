Climate Variability: The Impact on Ethiopia’s Economy & Persistent Poverty

Overview

The Federal Democratic Republic of Ethiopia is a landlocked country located in East Africa. As of 2025, it has a population of around 135 million. Ethiopia is not only the 10th-most populous country in the world but is also known for having one of the lowest GDPs per capita globally. Contributing to this are numerous contagious diseases that are worsened by poor sanitation and malnutrition. Over 58 million people, nearly half the population, do not have access to clean water as of 2023.

This development status makes the country more vulnerable to climate variability and change. Ethiopia has multiple climate zones that vary from hot and arid to cold and humid. However, the main environmental problems include droughts, floods, and water and air pollution, which lead to poor crop yields, land degradation, soil erosion, loss of biodiversity, and livestock depletion.

"What is the impact of climate variability on economic growth in Ethiopia, and how does it contribute to persistent poverty in an agriculture-dependent economy?"

Climate & Agriculture

Crops grown during the summer season are harvested between September and December and account for 90–95% of total annual production. The timing and amount of rainfall during the growing season are therefore critical for crop production, as 96% of cropland is rain-fed. This means that farmers rely heavily on the weather.

Agriculture is an essential driver of economic growth in the Ethiopian economy. In 2020, agriculture generated 34.5% of the total GDP. Beyond products, agricultural labor makes up around 85% of the total labor force. Experts predict warming temperatures in all four seasons, with an annual increase of 2.2°C by the 2050s.

Education

Work and school often compete for children’s time. In a recent survey, higher rainfall was negatively correlated with enrollment, especially for boys, who are often pulled out of school to assist with farm labor during productive periods.

  • Primary school enrollment: 57% for boys, 59% for girls.
  • Secondary school enrollment (ages 15-18): approximately 2.5%.
  • 71% of rural children aged 10–14 are employed.

Effect & NARDL

Climate change creates "poverty traps." An experiment showed that a 1% increase in temperature variability can decrease Ethiopian annual GDP by up to 4.5%.

The NARDL model shows that Ethiopia's economy responds more drastically to "bad" weather shocks than to "good" ones, meaning damage from a drought takes much longer to recover from than a good season helps.

Summary

Heavy reliance on rain-fed agriculture intensities vulnerabilities. Over a 50-year period, climate change may lead to 20% to 30% less average income for the nation.

  • Macro Strategy: Adaptation must be part of core economic planning.
  • Poverty Cycle: Shocks prevent family asset accumulation.
